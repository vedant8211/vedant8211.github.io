The Rich History of Uttar Pradesh

Uttar Pradesh, often called the heartland of India, has a history that stretches back to the most ancient periods of Indian civilization:

Ancient Period

The region that is now Uttar Pradesh was the center of ancient kingdoms and the birthplace of significant religious traditions. The earliest known kingdoms in the area were Kosala (with its capital at Ayodhya) and Kashi (with its capital at Varanasi), mentioned in ancient Vedic texts.

The region witnessed the rise of Buddhism and Jainism in the 6th century BCE. Lord Buddha delivered his first sermon at Sarnath near Varanasi after attaining enlightenment. The great empires of ancient India, including the Mauryas (322-185 BCE) and Guptas (320-550 CE), had their capitals in present-day Uttar Pradesh. Cities like Prayagraj (Allahabad), Varanasi, and Mathura became important centers of learning, religion, and culture.

Medieval Period

The medieval period saw the region coming under various dynasties:

  • Delhi Sultanate (1206-1526): Several dynasties ruled from Delhi, controlling large parts of Uttar Pradesh.
  • Mughal Empire (1526-1857): The Mughals established their capital at Agra and later at Delhi, bringing a golden age of art, architecture, and culture. Emperor Akbar built Fatehpur Sikri near Agra as his capital, while Shah Jahan constructed the iconic Taj Mahal.
  • Regional Kingdoms: Awadh (Oudh) emerged as a significant power centered in Lucknow under the Nawabs, who were initially governors under the Mughals but later became autonomous rulers.

Colonial Period

The British East India Company gradually expanded its control over the region:

  • 1764: The Battle of Buxar gave the British control over Bengal, Bihar, and parts of eastern Uttar Pradesh.
  • 1801: The Nawab of Awadh ceded large territories to the British.
  • 1856: The British annexed the Kingdom of Awadh.
  • 1857: The First War of Independence (Indian Rebellion) began in Meerut and spread across the region.

After 1857, the region came under direct British rule as the "North-Western Provinces and Oudh," later renamed the United Provinces of Agra and Oudh in 1902. The region was a center of India's freedom struggle, with significant movements like the Non-Cooperation Movement and Civil Disobedience Movement finding strong support here.

Post-Independence Era

After India's independence in 1947, the United Provinces was renamed "Uttar Pradesh" (meaning "Northern Province"). In 2000, the mountainous northwestern region was carved out to form a new state called Uttarakhand.

Today, Uttar Pradesh remains India's most populous state and continues to be significant in national politics, having produced several Prime Ministers. The state faces challenges of development, population density, and social issues, but also boasts a rich cultural heritage, vibrant traditions, and important centers of religion, education, and industry.

Top Places to Visit in Uttar Pradesh

1. Agra - Home of the Taj Mahal

A city synonymous with the magnificent monument to love:

  • Taj Mahal: A UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the New Seven Wonders of the World, this white marble mausoleum built by Emperor Shah Jahan for his beloved wife Mumtaz Mahal is the epitome of Mughal architecture.
  • Agra Fort: A massive red sandstone fort containing palaces, audience halls, and mosques, another UNESCO World Heritage Site.
  • Fatehpur Sikri: A deserted city built by Emperor Akbar, featuring a blend of Hindu and Islamic architectural styles.
  • Itimad-ud-Daulah (Baby Taj): A mausoleum that inspired the Taj Mahal, known for its delicate marble lattice work.
  • Mehtab Bagh: Gardens offering a spectacular view of the Taj Mahal from across the Yamuna River.

2. Varanasi (Banaras/Kashi)

One of the world's oldest continuously inhabited cities and Hinduism's holiest site:

  • Ghats: The city has 88 ghats (steps leading to the river), where pilgrims perform rituals, cremations take place, and the evening Ganga Aarti (river worship ceremony) is conducted.
  • Kashi Vishwanath Temple: One of the most famous Hindu temples dedicated to Lord Shiva.
  • Sarnath: Where Buddha delivered his first sermon, featuring the Dhamek Stupa and Archaeological Museum.
  • Banaras Hindu University: One of Asia's largest residential universities, with a beautiful campus including the Bharat Kala Bhavan museum.
  • Boat Ride on the Ganges: Experience sunrise or sunset from a boat on the sacred river.

3. Lucknow - The City of Nawabs

  • Bara Imambara: A grand complex built in 1784, famous for its architectural brilliance and the Bhulbhulaiya (labyrinth).
  • Chota Imambara: Known as the Palace of Lights due to its beautiful chandeliers and mirror work.
  • Rumi Darwaza: A magnificent gateway built in 1784, inspired by a gate in Istanbul.
  • Residency: Ruins of the British Residency, which was the site of the 1857 uprising.
  • Hazratganj: The city's upscale shopping district with colonial-era architecture.
  • Culinary Experience: Lucknow is famous for its exquisite Awadhi cuisine, including kebabs, biryani, and various bread varieties.

4. Ayodhya

  • Ram Janmabhoomi: The birthplace of Lord Rama according to Hindu tradition.
  • Hanuman Garhi: A temple dedicated to Lord Hanuman, situated on a hill.
  • Kanak Bhawan: A temple believed to have been gifted to Sita and Ram by Kaikeyi.
  • Sarayu River: The sacred river mentioned in the Ramayana, with ghats for bathing and worship.
  • Nageshwarnath Temple: An ancient temple established by Ram's son Kush.

5. Mathura and Vrindavan

  • Krishna Janmabhoomi Temple: Built around the prison cell where Lord Krishna is believed to have been born.
  • Banke Bihari Temple (Vrindavan): One of the most revered temples dedicated to Lord Krishna.
  • ISKCON Temple (Vrindavan): A modern temple complex dedicated to Krishna-Balaram.
  • Prem Mandir (Vrindavan): A spiritual complex depicting Krishna's life events through carved sculptures.
  • Govardhan Hill: A sacred hill linked to stories of Lord Krishna.
  • Holi Celebrations: Mathura and Vrindavan are famous for their spectacular Holi (festival of colors) celebrations.

6. Prayagraj (Allahabad)

  • Triveni Sangam: The confluence of three rivers - Ganga, Yamuna, and the mythical Saraswati, a key pilgrimage site.
  • Kumbh Mela: The world's largest religious gathering, held every 12 years (and Ardh Kumbh every 6 years).
  • Allahabad Fort: Built by Emperor Akbar in 1583, housing the Akshaya Vat (immortal banyan tree).
  • Anand Bhavan: The ancestral home of the Nehru family, now a museum.
  • Khusro Bagh: A beautiful Mughal garden with tombs of Prince Khusro and his family members.

7. Jhansi

  • Jhansi Fort: A fortress associated with Rani Lakshmibai, the legendary queen who fought against the British during the 1857 uprising.
  • Rani Mahal: The palace of Rani Lakshmibai, now an archaeological museum.
  • Jhansi Museum: Houses artifacts from the Chandela dynasty and the period of the 1857 uprising.
  • St. Jude's Shrine: A church built in Indo-Gothic style.

8. Dudhwa National Park

  • Wildlife Sanctuary: Home to tigers, leopards, rhinoceros, elephants, and numerous bird species.
  • Dudhwa Tiger Reserve: One of the most important tiger conservation areas in India.
  • Tharu Tribal Villages: Experience the culture of the indigenous Tharu people.

Cultural Heritage of Uttar Pradesh

Art and Architecture

  • Mughal Architecture: Exemplified by the Taj Mahal, Agra Fort, and Fatehpur Sikri.
  • Nawabi Architecture: Seen in the buildings of Lucknow, featuring distinctive elements like domes, arches, and intricate carvings.
  • Temple Architecture: From ancient temples in Varanasi to modern ones like the Birla Mandir in Jhansi.
  • Handicrafts: Including Chikankari embroidery from Lucknow, brassware from Moradabad, glassware from Firozabad, carpets from Bhadohi, and pottery from Khurja.

Music and Dance

  • Kathak: One of India's classical dance forms with its Lucknow Gharana (school).
  • Nautanki: A traditional folk theater that combines dialogue, music, and dance.
  • Rasiya: A form of folk music from the Braj region, often centered around the theme of Krishna's love.
  • Qawwali: A form of Sufi devotional music, popular in certain regions of UP.

Literature

  • Hindi Literature: Uttar Pradesh has contributed significantly to Hindi literature with writers like Premchand, Harivansh Rai Bachchan, and Mahadevi Verma.
  • Urdu Literature: The state has a rich tradition of Urdu poetry and prose, with luminaries like Mirza Ghalib, Mir Taqi Mir, and Josh Malihabadi.
  • Braj Bhasha: A dialect of Hindi with a rich literary tradition, especially in poetry related to Krishna.
  • Awadhi: The language of the epic "Ramcharitmanas" by Tulsidas.

Cuisine

Uttar Pradesh's cuisine is as diverse as its culture, with regional variations and specialties:

  • Awadhi Cuisine (Lucknow): Known for dum pukht (slow cooking in sealed containers), kebabs, biryani, and rich gravies.
  • Banaras (Varanasi) Cuisine: Famous for its sweet dishes, chaat, and thandai (a spiced milk drink).
  • Braj Cuisine (Mathura-Vrindavan): Known for its sweets, especially pedas and milk-based desserts.
  • Street Food: The state is famous for its chaat, samosas, kachoris, and jalebi.
  • Specific Dishes: Tunday kebabs, Lucknowi biryani, Banarasi paan, Mathura ke pede, and Agra ka petha (a sweet made from ash gourd).

Festivals of Uttar Pradesh

  • Diwali: Celebrated with great enthusiasm, especially in Ayodhya, where a grand Deepotsav (festival of lamps) is organized.
  • Holi: Famous celebrations in Mathura and Vrindavan, including Lathmar Holi where women playfully beat men with sticks.
  • Ram Navami: Celebrated with special fervor in Ayodhya, marking Lord Rama's birth.
  • Janmashtami: The birth of Lord Krishna is celebrated with great enthusiasm in Mathura and Vrindavan.
  • Kumbh Mela: The world's largest religious gathering, held in Prayagraj, Haridwar, Ujjain, and Nashik on a rotational basis.
  • Ganga Mahotsav (Varanasi): A five-day cultural festival celebrating the River Ganges.
  • Taj Mahotsav (Agra): A 10-day festival showcasing arts, crafts, culture, and cuisine of the region.
  • Lucknow Mahotsav: An annual festival highlighting the cultural heritage of Lucknow.

Best Time to Visit

October to March is the ideal time to visit Uttar Pradesh when the weather is pleasant and conducive for sightseeing. Summers (April to June) can be extremely hot with temperatures often exceeding 45Â°C in some parts. The monsoon season (July to September) brings respite from the heat but can cause flooding in some areas, particularly near the rivers.

Travel Tips:

  • Uttar Pradesh is well-connected by air, rail, and road. Major cities like Lucknow, Varanasi, and Agra have airports with good connectivity.
  • The state has an extensive railway network, making train travel a convenient option for moving between cities.
  • Dress modestly, especially when visiting religious sites. Remove shoes before entering temples and cover your head in certain religious places.
  • Be cautious about drinking water; stick to bottled water to avoid health issues.
  • Try the local cuisine, but be mindful of street food hygiene if you have a sensitive stomach.
  • Bargaining is common in markets, especially for handicrafts and souvenirs.
  • Photography may be restricted in some religious and historical sites, so always check for signs or ask permission.
  • The state has a rich cultural etiquette; respecting local customs and traditions will enhance your experience.
