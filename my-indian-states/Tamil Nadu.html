Tamil Nadu: Land of Temples and Ancient Culture

Quick Facts:

  • Capital: Chennai
  • Largest City: Chennai
  • Formation: November 1, 1956 (as Madras State); Renamed Tamil Nadu on January 14, 1969
  • Area: 130,058 km²
  • Language: Tamil (official), one of the world's oldest classical languages
  • Famous for: Ancient temples, classical arts, Dravidian architecture, silk sarees, cuisine, film industry (Kollywood)

The Rich History of Tamil Nadu

Tamil Nadu's history spans thousands of years, making it one of the oldest civilizations in the world:

Ancient Period

Archaeological evidence suggests human habitation in Tamil Nadu dates back to at least 15,000 BCE, with Neolithic sites discovered at various locations. The region was home to one of the oldest continuous civilizations in the world, collectively known as the Dravidian civilization.

The ancient history of Tamil Nadu is divided into three major periods dominated by powerful dynasties:

  • Sangam Period (c. 300 BCE - 300 CE): Named after the Sangam academies of poets and scholars, this period saw significant cultural and literary development. The Chera, Chola, and Pandya dynasties (collectively known as the Three Crowned Kings - Moovendar) ruled different parts of Tamil Nadu during this time.
  • Kalabhra Period (c. 300-600 CE): A relatively obscure period often referred to as the "Dark Age" in Tamil history.
  • Pallava Period (c. 600-900 CE): The Pallavas ruled from Kanchipuram and were great patrons of art and architecture. They introduced the Dravidian style of temple architecture, evident in the monuments at Mahabalipuram.

Medieval Period

The medieval period saw the emergence of more powerful dynasties:

  • Chola Empire (c. 850-1279 CE): The Cholas created one of India's most impressive empires, extending their rule to parts of Southeast Asia. Under rulers like Raja Raja Chola I and Rajendra Chola I, art, architecture, and literature flourished. The magnificent Brihadeeswarar Temple at Thanjavur was built during this period.
  • Pandya Revival (c. 1216-1345 CE): The Pandyas reasserted their power after the decline of the Cholas.
  • Vijayanagara Empire (1336-1646 CE): Though centered in present-day Karnataka, this empire controlled parts of Tamil Nadu and protected Hindu traditions during the rise of Islamic powers in North India.
  • Nayak Period (c. 1529-1736 CE): The Nayaks were governors appointed by the Vijayanagara Empire who became independent after its decline. The Madurai Nayaks particularly contributed to Tamil literature, art, and temple architecture.

Colonial Period

European powers established trading posts in Tamil Nadu from the 16th century:

  • Portuguese: First to arrive, establishing bases at San Thome (Chennai) and Tuticorin.
  • Dutch and Danish: Established trading posts at locations like Pulicat and Tranquebar.
  • French: Controlled Pondicherry (now Puducherry).
  • British: Founded Madras (now Chennai) in 1639. After the Carnatic Wars with the French, the British East India Company gained control of most of Tamil Nadu, which became part of the Madras Presidency.

Modern Period

Tamil Nadu played a significant role in India's freedom struggle. C. Rajagopalachari, S. Satyamurti, and V.O. Chidambaram Pillai were prominent freedom fighters from the region. The state also saw a parallel Self-Respect Movement led by Periyar E.V. Ramasamy, which focused on social reform, anti-Brahminism, and Tamil identity.

After India's independence in 1947, the Madras State was formed. Following the States Reorganization Act of 1956, it was reorganized based on linguistic boundaries. In 1969, it was renamed Tamil Nadu ("Land of Tamils") to better reflect its cultural identity.

Top Places to Visit in Tamil Nadu

1. Chennai - The Capital City

A vibrant metropolis blending tradition and modernity:

  • Marina Beach: The second-longest urban beach in the world.
  • Fort St. George: The first English fortress in India, now housing the Tamil Nadu Legislative Assembly.
  • Kapaleeshwarar Temple: A magnificent Dravidian-style temple dedicated to Lord Shiva.
  • Government Museum: One of the oldest museums in India with a rich collection of artifacts.
  • San Thome Basilica: Built over the tomb of St. Thomas the Apostle, one of the twelve disciples of Jesus.
  • Elliots Beach (Besant Nagar Beach): A quieter alternative to Marina Beach.

2. Mahabalipuram (Mamallapuram)

A UNESCO World Heritage Site known for its stone-carved monuments:

  • Shore Temple: A 7th-century temple overlooking the Bay of Bengal.
  • Arjuna's Penance: A massive rock relief depicting stories from Hindu mythology.
  • Five Rathas (Pancha Rathas): Five monolithic shrines, each carved from a single rock.
  • Krishna's Butter Ball: A massive balanced boulder that has perched on a hillside for centuries.
  • Mahabalipuram Beach: A beautiful coastline perfect for relaxation.

3. Thanjavur (Tanjore)

  • Brihadeeswarar Temple: A UNESCO World Heritage Site and masterpiece of Chola architecture, housing one of India's largest Shiva lingams.
  • Thanjavur Palace: The official residence of the Bhonsle family, featuring the Saraswati Mahal Library with over 30,000 rare manuscripts.
  • Thanjavur Art Gallery: Houses a collection of bronze and stone sculptures from the 7th to 18th centuries.
  • Schwartz Church: A 18th-century church built by Reverend C.F. Schwartz.

4. Madurai

  • Meenakshi Amman Temple: A spectacular temple complex with 14 towering gopurams (gateway towers) covered in thousands of colorful sculptures.
  • Thirumalai Nayakkar Palace: A 17th-century palace showcasing Indo-Saracenic architecture.
  • Gandhi Memorial Museum: Housed in the historic Tamukkam Palace, displaying Gandhi's bloodstained dhoti from his assassination.
  • Alagar Kovil: A temple dedicated to Lord Vishnu located in the scenic Alagar Hills.
  • Vandiyur Mariamman Teppakulam: A massive temple tank that hosts the Float Festival.

5. Ooty (Udhagamandalam)

  • Nilgiri Mountain Railway: A UNESCO World Heritage Site, this toy train offers a scenic journey through the mountains.
  • Botanical Gardens: Established in 1848, featuring over 1,000 plant species.
  • Ooty Lake: An artificial lake created in 1824 offering boating opportunities.
  • Doddabetta Peak: The highest point in the Nilgiri Mountains with panoramic views.
  • Tea Gardens: Expansive tea plantations with factory tours available.

6. Kanyakumari

  • Thiruvalluvar Statue: A 133-foot tall statue of the Tamil poet and philosopher.
  • Vivekananda Rock Memorial: Built on the rock where Swami Vivekananda meditated.
  • Kanyakumari Temple: Dedicated to Goddess Parvati as Devi Kanya Kumari.
  • Sangam Point: The confluence of the Arabian Sea, Bay of Bengal, and Indian Ocean.
  • Sunset and Sunrise Views: One of the few places in India where both sunrise and sunset can be viewed over the ocean.

7. Rameshwaram

  • Ramanathaswamy Temple: Famous for its long corridors with over 1,200 intricately carved pillars.
  • Pamban Bridge: India's first sea bridge connecting Rameshwaram to the mainland.
  • Dhanushkodi: A ghost town destroyed by a cyclone in 1964, with beaches where the Bay of Bengal meets the Indian Ocean.
  • Adam's Bridge (Ram Setu): A chain of limestone shoals between India and Sri Lanka mentioned in the epic Ramayana.

8. Kodaikanal

  • Kodaikanal Lake: A star-shaped artificial lake offering boating experiences.
  • Bryant Park: A botanical garden with a variety of flowers and plants.
  • Coaker's Walk: A pedestrian path offering panoramic views of the plains below.
  • Pillar Rocks: Three giant rock pillars standing side by side at a height of 400 feet.
  • Kurinji Andavar Temple: Famous for the Kurinji flower that blooms once every 12 years.

9. Chettinad Region

  • Chettinad Mansions: Opulent mansions built by the wealthy Chettiar merchant community.
  • Athangudi Palace Tiles: Traditional handmade tiles unique to this region.
  • Chettinad Cuisine: Known for its aromatic and spicy flavors.
  • Kanadukathan: A village with well-preserved Chettinad architecture.

Cultural Heritage of Tamil Nadu

Classical Arts

  • Bharatanatyam: One of India's oldest classical dance forms, originating in Tamil Nadu.
  • Carnatic Music: South Indian classical music with a rich theoretical and practical tradition.
  • Tanjore Painting: A classical South Indian painting style characterized by rich, flat colors and gold foil overlays.
  • Kolam: Traditional geometric patterns drawn with rice powder at the entrance of homes.

Literature and Language

Tamil is one of the world's oldest languages with a rich literary tradition:

  • Sangam Literature: The earliest Tamil literature dating back to 300 BCE - 300 CE.
  • Thirukkural: A classic Tamil text by Thiruvalluvar containing 1,330 couplets on ethics, political and economic matters, and love.
  • Silappadikaram and Manimekalai: Ancient Tamil epics from the 5th-6th centuries.
  • Modern Tamil Literature: Including works by Subramania Bharati, Kalki Krishnamurthy, and others.

Architecture

  • Dravidian Temple Architecture: Characterized by pyramidal towers (gopurams), stone carvings, and vast temple complexes.
  • Temple Tanks (Teppakulam): Large square or rectangular water reservoirs used for rituals and festivals.
  • Colonial Architecture: British, French, and Portuguese influences seen in buildings across the state, especially in Chennai and Puducherry.
  • Chettinad Architecture: Unique style featuring mansions with materials sourced from around the world.

Cuisine

Tamil cuisine is known for its use of rice, lentils, and spices:

  • Staples: Idli, Dosa, Vada, Uttapam, Pongal, Sambar, Rasam.
  • Traditional Meals: Served on banana leaves with rice at the center surrounded by various curries and sides.
  • Regional Specialties: Chettinad cuisine (spicy and aromatic), Kongunadu cuisine (focusing on preserved foods), and Nanjilnadu cuisine (coastal flavors).
  • Sweets and Desserts: Payasam, Mysore Pak, Jangiri, Adhirasam, and Paniyaram.
  • Filter Coffee: A traditional South Indian coffee preparation method.

Textiles and Handicrafts

  • Kanchipuram Silk Sarees: Handwoven silk sarees with gold thread work, considered among the finest in India.
  • Madurai Sungudi: Traditional cotton sarees with tie-and-dye patterns.
  • Bronze Casting: Especially the lost-wax process used for creating Chola bronzes.
  • Stone Carving: A tradition dating back thousands of years, visible in temples across the state.
  • Palm Leaf Manuscripts: Ancient texts and art created on dried palm leaves.

Festivals of Tamil Nadu

  • Pongal (January): A four-day harvest festival thanking the sun god, with special dishes prepared and cattle honored.
  • Thai Pusam (January-February): A festival honoring Lord Murugan, featuring kavadi (physical burdens) carried by devotees.
  • Mahamaham (once every 12 years): A grand temple festival in Kumbakonam where devotees bathe in the sacred tank.
  • Chittirai Festival (April-May): Celebrates the celestial wedding of Goddess Meenakshi and Lord Sundareswarar in Madurai.
  • Aadi Perukku (July-August): A celebration of water bodies, especially the Cauvery River.
  • Navaratri (September-October): Features golu (display of dolls) and special music and dance performances.
  • Chennai Music Season (December-January): One of the world's largest music festivals featuring hundreds of Carnatic music performances.

Best Time to Visit

October to March is generally the best time to visit Tamil Nadu when the weather is relatively cooler and pleasant. The summer months (April to June) can be extremely hot, especially in the plains. The northeastern monsoon affects Tamil Nadu between October and December, bringing significant rainfall particularly to coastal areas.

Travel Tips:

  • Dress modestly when visiting temples, with shoulders and knees covered. Remove shoes before entering.
  • Try the traditional South Indian meals served on banana leaves for an authentic dining experience.
  • Learn a few basic Tamil phrases to connect with locals, who greatly appreciate the effort.
  • Stay hydrated, especially during summer months when temperatures can soar.
  • Plan your visit around festivals to experience the rich cultural traditions of Tamil Nadu.
  • Be prepared for occasional power outages, especially in smaller towns and rural areas.
  • Consider hiring a guide when visiting temples to better understand their architectural and mythological significance.
  • Use public transportation like state buses and local trains for an authentic experience; app-based taxis are widely available in cities.
