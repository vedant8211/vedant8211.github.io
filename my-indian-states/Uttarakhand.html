The Rich History of Uttarakhand

Uttarakhand, often referred to as "Devbhumi" (Land of Gods), has a rich and ancient history embedded in mythology, spirituality, and cultural heritage:

Ancient Period

The region's history dates back to ancient times, with archaeological evidence suggesting human habitation since prehistoric periods. Uttarakhand finds mention in ancient Hindu texts, including the Vedas, Puranas, and epics like the Mahabharata. According to Hindu mythology, the Pandavas passed through this region on their way to heaven, and many places here are associated with legends of gods and sages.

The region was known as Kedarkhand, Manaskhand, and Himavant in different ancient texts. It was a center of spiritual learning and meditation, with sages and ascetics establishing ashrams throughout the area.

Medieval Period

During the medieval period, the region was divided into two distinct cultural-geographical entities:

  • Garhwal Kingdom: Established in the 8th century, the Garhwal region was initially ruled by the Katyuri dynasty. Later, the Panwar dynasty unified the many small fiefdoms and established the Garhwal Kingdom with Srinagar (now in Pauri Garhwal) as its capital.
  • Kumaon Kingdom: The eastern part of Uttarakhand, known as Kumaon, was ruled by the Chand dynasty from the 11th century until the early 19th century, with Champawat and later Almora as their capitals.

Both kingdoms maintained their independence despite invasions and maintained unique cultural traditions that are still practiced today.

Colonial Period

The Gorkha rulers of Nepal invaded and conquered large parts of Garhwal and Kumaon in the late 18th century. However, their rule was short-lived. Following the Anglo-Nepalese War (1814-1816), the British East India Company gained control of the region through the Treaty of Sugauli.

Under British rule, the region was part of the United Provinces of Agra and Oudh (later renamed Uttar Pradesh after independence). The British established hill stations like Mussoorie, Nainital, and Lansdowne as summer retreats. During this period, the region also saw significant social reforms and the emergence of freedom fighters who contributed to India's independence movement.

Post-Independence Era and State Formation

After India's independence in 1947, the region became part of Uttar Pradesh. However, the cultural, geographical, and developmental differences from the plains led to demands for a separate state. The Uttarakhand movement gained momentum in the 1990s, particularly after the Rampur Tiraha firing incident in 1994, where police fired on protesters.

Finally, on November 9, 2000, Uttarakhand (initially called Uttaranchal; renamed in 2007) was carved out of Uttar Pradesh as India's 27th state. Dehradun was designated as the state's provisional capital, while Gairsain in Chamoli district was declared the summer capital in 2020, fulfilling a long-standing demand.

Since its formation, Uttarakhand has focused on harnessing its tourism potential, preserving its rich cultural heritage, and addressing unique developmental challenges posed by its mountainous geography.

Top Places to Visit in Uttarakhand

1. Dehradun - The Gateway to Uttarakhand

The capital city nestled in the Doon Valley:

  • Robber's Cave (Guchhupani): A unique natural cave formation with a stream running through it.
  • Mindrolling Monastery: One of the largest Buddhist centers in India with a 220-foot Great Stupa.
  • Forest Research Institute: A colonial-era building with extensive forestry museums and beautiful grounds.
  • Rajaji National Park: Located nearby, home to elephants, tigers, and diverse bird species.
  • Sahastradhara: "Thousand-fold spring" with sulphur water springs believed to have medicinal properties.
  • Tapkeshwar Temple: An ancient Shiva temple built inside a natural cave.

2. Rishikesh - The Yoga Capital

A spiritual center situated on the banks of the Ganges:

  • Laxman Jhula and Ram Jhula: Iconic suspension bridges across the Ganges.
  • Triveni Ghat: A sacred bathing ghat where evening Ganga Aarti takes place.
  • The Beatles Ashram: Where the famous band stayed in 1968 to learn Transcendental Meditation.
  • Neelkanth Mahadev Temple: An important Shiva temple located 32 km from Rishikesh at an elevation of 1,330 meters.
  • Adventure Activities: White water rafting, bungee jumping, cliff jumping, and zipline.
  • Yoga and Meditation Centers: Numerous ashrams and institutes offering yoga courses and retreats.

3. Haridwar

One of the seven holiest places in Hinduism:

  • Har Ki Pauri: A sacred ghat where the Ganga Aarti is performed every evening.
  • Mansa Devi Temple: A temple accessible by cable car, offering panoramic views of the city.
  • Chandi Devi Temple: Another hilltop temple reached by cable car or a trek.
  • Kumbh Mela: The largest religious gathering in the world, held every 12 years (and Ardh Kumbh every 6 years).
  • Maya Devi Temple: One of the Shakti Peethas, dedicated to Goddess Maya.

4. Mussoorie - The Queen of Hills

  • Mall Road: The main shopping and social street with colonial architecture and scenic views.
  • Kempty Falls: A popular waterfall and picnic spot.
  • Lal Tibba: The highest point in Mussoorie with telescopes for viewing Himalayan peaks.
  • Gun Hill: The second highest point, accessible by cable car.
  • Cloud's End: A colonial-era estate marking the geographical end of Mussoorie.
  • Sir George Everest's House: The former residence of the Surveyor General of India after whom Mount Everest is named.

5. Nainital

  • Naini Lake: The emerald eye-shaped lake at the center of the town.
  • Mall Road: The bustling main street for shopping and dining.
  • Naina Devi Temple: An important temple dedicated to Goddess Naina Devi.
  • Snow View Point: Offering panoramic views of snow-capped Himalayan peaks.
  • Tiffin Top (Dorothy's Seat): A viewpoint offering bird's eye view of Nainital.
  • Eco Cave Gardens: A network of interconnected caves with educational value.

6. The Char Dham

The four most sacred Hindu pilgrimage sites in Uttarakhand:

  • Yamunotri: The source of the Yamuna River, with a temple dedicated to Goddess Yamuna.
  • Gangotri: The origin of the Ganges River, with a temple dedicated to Goddess Ganga.
  • Kedarnath: Home to one of the 12 Jyotirlingas (self-manifested Shiva lingas), accessible only by a 16 km trek.
  • Badrinath: A temple dedicated to Lord Vishnu, located between the Nar and Narayana mountain ranges.

7. Valley of Flowers National Park

  • UNESCO World Heritage Site: Known for its meadows of endemic alpine flowers and diverse flora.
  • Trekking: A popular trek from Govindghat, best visited during the monsoon (July-September) when the flowers are in full bloom.
  • Hemkund Sahib: A sacred Sikh gurudwara located nearby at an altitude of 4,632 meters.
  • Biodiversity: Home to rare and endangered animals including the Asiatic black bear, snow leopard, and blue sheep.

8. Jim Corbett National Park

  • India's Oldest National Park: Established in 1936 to protect the Bengal tiger.
  • Wildlife Safari: Home to tigers, elephants, leopards, and over 600 species of birds.
  • Dhikala: The most popular safari zone with grasslands and wetlands.
  • Corbett Falls: A scenic waterfall located near the park.
  • Corbett Museum: Formerly the winter home of Jim Corbett, now a museum dedicated to his life and work.

9. Auli

  • Ski Destination: One of India's premier skiing destinations with slopes covered in snow during winter.
  • Auli Ropeway: One of the longest cable cars in Asia, connecting Joshimath to Auli.
  • Panoramic Views: Spectacular views of Nanda Devi, Kamet, Mana Parvat, and other Himalayan peaks.
  • Artificial Lake: Used for snowmaking during the ski season.
  • Trekking: Base for treks to destinations like Gurson Bugyal and Kuari Pass.

Cultural Heritage of Uttarakhand

Traditional Arts and Crafts

  • Aipan: A traditional art form using rice paste to create auspicious patterns and motifs.
  • Ringal Crafts: Items made from ringal bamboo, including baskets, mats, and utensils.
  • Wood Carving: Intricate designs carved on doors, windows, and household items.
  • Copper Work: Handcrafted copper utensils, particularly from Almora and Dehradun.
  • Woolen Crafts: Hand-knitted sweaters, caps, socks, and pankhi (traditional Garhwali shawls).

Folk Music and Dance

  • Langvir Nritya: A dance depicting war scenes performed by men in the Garhwal region.
  • Chholiya: A sword dance performed during auspicious occasions, especially weddings.
  • Jhora: A popular recreational dance in the Kumaon region.
  • Barada Nati: A traditional dance popular in the Jaunsar-Bawar region.
  • Folk Songs: Including Mangal, Basanti, Khuded, and Jagar, often accompanied by traditional instruments like dhol, damau, and hudka.

Fairs and Festivals

  • Kumbh Mela: Held in Haridwar every 12 years, one of the largest religious gatherings in the world.
  • Nanda Devi Raj Jat: A pilgrimage held once every 12 years in honor of Goddess Nanda Devi.
  • Phool Dei: A springtime festival when young girls collect flowers and give blessings.
  • Harela: An agricultural festival marking the beginning of the sowing season.
  • Uttarayani Mela: Held in January at various locations in Kumaon, especially Bageshwar.
  • Ganga Dussehra: Celebrates the descent of the Ganges from heaven to earth.

Cuisine

Uttarakhand cuisine is simple, nutritious, and adapted to the mountain lifestyle:

  • Staple Foods: Mandua ki roti (finger millet flatbread), jhangora ki kheer (barnyard millet pudding), and locally grown rice.
  • Regional Specialties: Kafuli (a spinach preparation), aloo ke gutke (spiced potatoes), and bhang ki chutney (hemp seed chutney).
  • Garhwali Cuisine: Features dishes like chainsoo (a black gram preparation) and phaanu (a lentil dish).
  • Kumaoni Cuisine: Known for bhatt ki churkani (a preparation of black soybean), and bal mithai (a chocolate-like sweet).
  • Seasonal Specialties: Wild lingri (fiddlehead fern) and various wild mushrooms used in traditional cooking.

Spiritual Significance

Uttarakhand is called "Devbhumi" (Land of Gods) for good reason:

  • Panch Kedar: Five sacred Shiva temples - Kedarnath, Madhyamaheshwar, Tungnath, Rudranath, and Kalpeshwar.
  • Panch Badri: Five Badrinath temples - Badrinath, Yogadhyan Badri, Bridha Badri, Adi Badri, and Bhavishya Badri.
  • Panch Prayag: The five confluences of the Alaknanda River - Devprayag, Rudraprayag, Karnaprayag, Nandaprayag, and Vishnuprayag.
  • Spiritual Centers: Numerous ashrams and yoga centers, especially in Rishikesh and Haridwar.
  • Living Traditions: Ancient spiritual practices and rituals that continue to this day.

Best Time to Visit

The best time to visit Uttarakhand depends on the region and activities planned:

  • March to June: Ideal for visiting hill stations and undertaking treks.
  • July to September: Monsoon season - beautiful but prone to landslides. The Valley of Flowers blooms during this period.
  • October to February: Clear views of snow-capped mountains. December to February is perfect for winter sports in places like Auli.
  • Char Dham Yatra: The pilgrimage routes are typically open from late April/early May to October/November, depending on weather conditions.

Travel Tips:

  • Uttarakhand has varying altitudes, so pack accordingly with layers even in summer.
  • Many areas are ecologically sensitive, so practice responsible tourism and avoid plastic.
  • Road conditions can be challenging in mountainous areas, especially during monsoons.
  • For wildlife safaris in Jim Corbett, book well in advance, especially during peak season.
  • The Char Dham Yatra requires physical fitness, especially for Kedarnath which involves a significant trek.
  • Respect local customs and dress modestly, especially when visiting religious sites.
  • Carry necessary medications if trekking to high altitudes, and be aware of symptoms of altitude sickness.
  • Mobile connectivity can be limited in remote areas, so inform someone about your itinerary.
