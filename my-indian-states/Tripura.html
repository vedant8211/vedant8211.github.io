The Rich History of Tripura

Tripura has a fascinating history spanning thousands of years, with legends and historical records intertwining:

Ancient Period

According to the Rajmala, the royal chronicle of Tripura's kings, the kingdom of Tripura was established around 2nd century CE. The name "Tripura" has various origin theories, including derivation from "Twipra" (the indigenous Tripuri people) or from "Tripura Sundari," a manifestation of Goddess Parvati.

Ancient rock carvings, stone sculptures, and archaeological findings suggest that the region had developed civilizations. Tripura finds mention in the Mahabharata, the Puranas, and other ancient texts, indicating its historical significance.

The Manikya Dynasty

The most notable period of Tripura's history was under the Manikya dynasty, which ruled from the 13th century until 1949. The dynasty was established by Ratna Fa (Ratna Manikya) and continued for over 500 years.

During the 15th century, Tripura reached its zenith under Maharaja Dhanya Manikya, who expanded the kingdom's territory significantly. The Manikya rulers patronized art, literature, and cultural activities, leading to the development of a rich cultural heritage. They also maintained diplomatic relations with neighboring kingdoms and empires, including the Mughals.

Colonial Influence

With the expansion of British influence in the 18th and 19th centuries, Tripura, like many other princely states, gradually came under British colonial influence. In 1871, the British recognized Tripura as a native state. During this period, the kingdom faced economic challenges due to changing trade patterns and administrative policies.

Despite the challenges, rulers like Maharaja Bir Chandra Manikya (1862-1896) and Maharaja Radha Kishore Manikya (1896-1909) implemented progressive reforms, established schools, introduced modern healthcare, and promoted cultural activities. Maharaja Bir Bikram Kishore Manikya (1923-1947), often called the "Father of Modern Tripura," was particularly notable for his modernization efforts.

Post-Independence Era

After India's independence in 1947, there was uncertainty about Tripura's status. Maharani Kanchan Prabha Devi, acting as regent for her minor son Kirit Bikram Kishore Manikya, signed the Instrument of Accession with the Indian Union on October 15, 1949. Tripura became a Part C state and later a Union Territory in 1956.

On January 21, 1972, Tripura was granted full statehood, meeting a long-standing demand of its people. The period after statehood saw significant demographic changes due to the influx of refugees from East Pakistan (now Bangladesh) following the Partition and later the Bangladesh Liberation War of 1971.

In the late 20th century, Tripura faced insurgency issues, but peace efforts and development initiatives have significantly improved the situation. Today, Tripura focuses on sustainable development, cultural preservation, and leveraging its strategic location for international trade with neighboring Bangladesh.

Top Places to Visit in Tripura

1. Agartala - The Capital City

A blend of royal heritage and modern development:

  • Ujjayanta Palace: The former royal palace of Tripura's Manikya dynasty, now housing the Tripura State Museum with its rich collection of artifacts.
  • Neermahal Palace: A stunning white palace situated in the middle of Rudrasagar Lake, built as a summer residence for Maharaja Bir Bikram Kishore Manikya.
  • Jagannath Temple: A temple dedicated to Lord Jagannath, showcasing a blend of Bengali and traditional Tripura architectural styles.
  • Fourteen Gods Temple (Chaturdasha Devata Mandir): An ancient temple dedicated to fourteen Hindu deities, representing the traditional tribal faith.
  • Heritage Park: A beautiful park showcasing Tripura's rich cultural heritage and tribal life.
  • Tripura Sundari Temple: One of the 51 Shakti Peethas in Hindu tradition, located in Udaipur, 55 km from Agartala.

2. Udaipur

The former capital of Tripura state, known as "Chittakoot" in ancient times:

  • Tripura Sundari Temple: Also known as Tripureshwari Temple, it's one of the holiest Hindu shrines in Northeast India.
  • Bhuvaneshwari Temple: An ancient temple with beautiful architecture.
  • Chabimura: Massive rock carvings of Hindu deities on the steep mountain walls along the Gomati River.
  • Mahamuni Buddhist Temple: A significant Buddhist pilgrimage site.

3. Jampui Hills

  • Orange Gardens: The highest hill range in Tripura, famous for its orange orchards and panoramic views.
  • Eden Tourist Lodge: Located on the Vanghmun hilltop, offering spectacular views of the surrounding landscape.
  • Tribal Villages: Experience the unique culture of the Lushai (Mizo) tribes who inhabit the area.
  • Sunset Point: Witness breathtaking sunsets over the hills.

4. Sepahijala Wildlife Sanctuary

  • Diverse Wildlife: Home to clouded leopards, capped langurs, spectacled monkeys, and various bird species.
  • Clouded Leopard National Park: A sanctuary within Sepahijala dedicated to clouded leopards, the state animal of Tripura.
  • Botanical Garden: Features a wide variety of plant species native to the region.
  • Boating: Enjoy boating in the sanctuary's lake.

5. Dumboor Lake

  • Largest Lake: A reservoir with 48 islands, named after its dumbbell-like shape.
  • Bird Watching: A paradise for bird watchers with numerous migratory and resident bird species.
  • Tribal Culture: Surrounded by villages of the Reang tribe, offering cultural insights.
  • Water Sports: Activities like boating and fishing.

6. Unakoti

  • Rock Carvings: An ancient pilgrimage site with massive rock-cut sculptures dating back to the 7th-9th centuries.
  • Unakoti Bas-reliefs: Features colossal carvings of Lord Shiva, Ganesha, and other Hindu deities.
  • Archaeological Mystery: The name "Unakoti" means "one less than a crore," referring to the legend that there are 9,999,999 rock-cut figures.
  • Rainy Season Waterfall: During monsoons, the water cascades over the rock carvings, creating a spectacular sight.

7. Pilak

  • Archaeological Site: Excavations have revealed Buddhist and Hindu sculptures from the 8th-9th centuries.
  • Cultural Fusion: Artifacts show a blend of Buddhist, Hindu, and traditional tribal influences.
  • Historical Significance: Evidence of Tripura's historical connection to the Pan-Asian Buddhist cultural sphere.

Cultural Heritage of Tripura

Tribal Communities

Tripura is home to 19 tribal communities, each with distinct cultural identities:

  • Tripuri: The largest tribal group, with their own language (Kokborok) and cultural traditions.
  • Reang (Bru): Known for their unique dance forms and traditional bamboo crafts.
  • Jamatia: One of the most organized tribal communities, known for their disciplined social structure.
  • Chakma: Known for their colorful textiles and Buddhist traditions.
  • Other Tribes: Include Mog, Garo, Kuki, Lushai (Mizo), Halam, Munda, Orang, and Santhal, each contributing to Tripura's rich cultural mosaic.

Traditional Dance and Music

  • Hojagiri: A spectacular dance performed by the Reang tribe, where women balance earthen lamps on their heads while dancing on bottles.
  • Garia Dance: Performed during Garia Puja, a major festival of the Tripuri people.
  • Lebang Dance: A traditional dance of the Tripuri community performed during the jhum cultivation season.
  • Mamita Dance: Performed by Chakma communities during their New Year celebrations.
  • Musical Instruments: Traditional instruments include the Sarinda, Chongpreng, Kham (drum), Sumui (flute), and Khengrong (a type of violin).

Arts and Crafts

  • Bamboo Crafts: Tripura excels in bamboo artistry, creating furniture, decorative items, utensils, and agricultural tools.
  • Cane Crafts: Similar to bamboo work but using cane, producing items like furniture and baskets.
  • Textile Weaving: Traditional handlooms produce rignai and risa (traditional Tripuri attire), along with other fabrics with distinctive patterns.
  • Wood Carving: Skilled artisans create intricate sculptures and household items from local wood.
  • Terracotta: Clay pottery and figurines with traditional designs and motifs.

Cuisine

Tripura's cuisine is a flavorful blend of Bengali, North Indian, and traditional tribal influences:

  • Berma: Fermented fish, a staple in traditional tribal cuisine.
  • Chakhwi: A bamboo shoot dish popular among tribal communities.
  • Wahan Mosdeng: A spicy chutney made with roasted tomato, garlic, and chili.
  • Muya Awandru: A traditional dish made with bamboo shoots and pork.
  • Bangwi: A traditional rice beer prepared by various tribal communities.
  • Bengali Influences: Fish curries, rice, and Bengali sweets are also common in Tripura's cuisine.

Festivals of Tripura

  • Garia Puja (April): A major festival of the Tripuri people celebrating good harvest and prosperity.
  • Kharchi Puja (July): A week-long royal tribal festival dedicated to the fourteen gods of Tripura.
  • Ker Puja (July-August): A unique festival for the welfare and protection of the people.
  • Durga Puja (September-October): Celebrated with great enthusiasm across the state, reflecting Bengali cultural influence.
  • Diwali and Kali Puja (October-November): Widely celebrated with lights, fireworks, and religious rituals.
  • Orange Festival (November-December): Celebrated in Jampui Hills, showcasing the region's famous orange harvest.
  • Tribal Festivals: Various tribal communities celebrate their own festivals like Biju (Chakma New Year), Sangrai (Mog New Year), and Hozagiri Festival.

Best Time to Visit

October to March is the ideal time to visit Tripura when the weather is pleasant and conducive for sightseeing. Summers (April to June) can be hot and humid, while the monsoon season (July to September) brings heavy rainfall that may affect travel plans, though it also makes the landscape lush and beautiful.

Travel Tips:

  • Agartala has air connectivity with major Indian cities, and the Agartala railway station connects the state with the rest of India.
  • Inner Line Permit (ILP) is not required for domestic tourists, but foreign nationals need to register with the Foreigner Registration Office.
  • Local transportation includes auto-rickshaws, cycle rickshaws, and government buses.
  • Tripura is generally safe, but it's advisable to check the current situation in remote areas before traveling.
  • Try local bamboo shoot dishes and traditional tribal cuisine for an authentic experience.
  • The state has good mobile network coverage in urban areas, but connectivity might be limited in remote regions.
  • Carry mosquito repellent, especially during and after the monsoon season.
  • Bamboo handicrafts make excellent souvenirs to take back home.
