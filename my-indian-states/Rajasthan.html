Rajasthan: The Land of Kings

Quick Facts:

  • Capital: Jaipur
  • Largest City: Jaipur
  • Formation: November 1, 1956 (present boundaries)
  • Area: 342,239 km² (India's largest state by area)
  • Language: Hindi, Rajasthani (with various dialects including Marwari, Mewari, Dhundhari)
  • Famous for: Majestic forts and palaces, vibrant culture, Thar Desert, traditional arts and crafts, royal heritage

The Rich History of Rajasthan

Rajasthan, literally meaning "Land of Kings," has a fascinating history spanning several millennia:

Ancient Period

Archaeological evidence suggests human settlements in Rajasthan date back to the Indus Valley Civilization (3300-1300 BCE). Kalibangan in northern Rajasthan was an important provincial center of the civilization. The region was part of various ancient kingdoms and republics mentioned in Mahabharata and Buddhist literature.

Medieval Era and Rajput Kingdoms

The medieval period saw the rise of powerful Rajput clans such as the Chauhans, Paramaras, Guhilas, Sisodiyas, Rathores, and Kachchwahas, who established various kingdoms across the region. The Rajputs became known for their fierce independence, martial valor, and chivalric code.

Notable Rajput kingdoms included:

  • Mewar (Udaipur): Ruled by the Sisodiyas, known for rulers like Maharana Pratap who resisted Mughal dominance
  • Marwar (Jodhpur): Established by the Rathore clan
  • Amber/Jaipur: Founded by the Kachchwahas who formed close alliances with the Mughals
  • Bikaner: Another Rathore kingdom established in the arid northwestern region

Mughal Period

The 16th century marked the expansion of Mughal influence in Rajasthan. Emperor Akbar established matrimonial alliances with several Rajput houses, incorporating them into the imperial administration. However, some kingdoms like Mewar under Maharana Pratap (1572-1597) fiercely resisted Mughal domination. The blend of Rajput and Mughal cultures led to remarkable developments in architecture, art, and cuisine.

Maratha Influence and British Era

As Mughal power declined in the 18th century, Maratha influence grew in Rajasthan. Eventually, most Rajput states signed subsidiary alliances with the British East India Company, maintaining their internal autonomy while acknowledging British suzerainty. Unlike many other regions of India, most of Rajasthan remained under indirect rule through princely states rather than direct British administration.

Post-Independence Era

After India's independence in 1947, the process of integrating the princely states began. Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel, India's first Deputy Prime Minister, played a crucial role in this process. The Rajputana Agency was reorganized as Rajasthan state in stages, culminating in the Greater Rajasthan in 1956 with Jaipur as its capital.

Top Places to Visit in Rajasthan

1. Jaipur - The Pink City

Rajasthan's capital, known for its distinctive pink-colored buildings and grand royal heritage:

  • Amber Fort: A majestic hilltop fortress with stunning Rajput architecture, mirror work, and the famous elephant ride to the entrance.
  • Hawa Mahal (Palace of Winds): A five-story palace with 953 small windows designed to allow royal ladies to observe street festivities without being seen.
  • City Palace: A complex of courtyards, gardens, and buildings that remains the home of the royal family.
  • Jantar Mantar: A UNESCO World Heritage site featuring the world's largest stone sundial and other astronomical instruments.
  • Jal Mahal (Water Palace): A palace seemingly floating in the middle of Man Sagar Lake.
  • Albert Hall Museum: The oldest museum in Rajasthan with a rich collection of artifacts.

2. Udaipur - The City of Lakes

Often called the "Venice of the East" and known as one of India's most romantic cities:

  • City Palace: A massive complex on the banks of Lake Pichola with stunning views, ornate interiors, and museums.
  • Lake Pichola: A beautiful artificial lake with two island palaces - Jag Niwas (Lake Palace) and Jag Mandir.
  • Saheliyon Ki Bari: A ornamental pleasure garden with fountains, kiosks, marble elephants, and a delightful lotus pool.
  • Bagore Ki Haveli: An old haveli converted into a museum showcasing Rajasthani culture and costumes.
  • Jagdish Temple: A large Hindu temple in Indo-Aryan style dedicated to Lord Vishnu.
  • Monsoon Palace (Sajjangarh): A hilltop palace offering spectacular sunset views over the lakes and city.

3. Jodhpur - The Blue City

  • Mehrangarh Fort: One of India's largest forts, perched 400 feet above the city with intricate palaces and expansive courtyards.
  • Umaid Bhawan Palace: One of the world's largest private residences, part of which is a luxury hotel and part a museum.
  • Jaswant Thada: A white marble memorial built in memory of Maharaja Jaswant Singh II.
  • Clock Tower and Sardar Market: A vibrant traditional market area excellent for shopping and experiencing local culture.
  • Blue Houses: Explore the maze-like lanes of the old city with its characteristic blue-painted houses.

4. Jaisalmer - The Golden City

  • Jaisalmer Fort: A UNESCO World Heritage Site, this "living fort" contains homes, hotels, shops, and temples with golden sandstone architecture.
  • Patwon Ki Haveli: The most elaborate and magnificent of Jaisalmer's havelis (mansions), known for intricate carvings.
  • Gadisar Lake: A picturesque man-made lake surrounded by temples and ghats.
  • Sam Sand Dunes: Experience desert landscapes with camel safaris and cultural performances.
  • Bada Bagh: A garden complex with royal cenotaphs (chhatris) of Jaisalmer rulers.

5. Pushkar

  • Pushkar Lake: A sacred lake with 52 ghats where pilgrims bathe to cleanse sins.
  • Brahma Temple: One of the very few temples in the world dedicated to Lord Brahma.
  • Pushkar Camel Fair: One of the world's largest camel fairs, held annually in November.
  • Savitri Temple: Situated on a hilltop offering panoramic views of the town.

6. Ranthambore National Park

  • Wildlife Safari: One of the best places in India to spot tigers in their natural habitat.
  • Ranthambore Fort: A UNESCO World Heritage Site with ancient temples and stunning views of the park.
  • Padam Talao: A large lake where wildlife often comes to drink.
  • Jogi Mahal: A former forest rest house at the foot of the fort.

7. Bikaner

  • Junagarh Fort: An unassailable fortress that has never been conquered.
  • Karni Mata Temple (Rat Temple): Famous for thousands of rats that are worshipped.
  • Lalgarh Palace: A magnificent red sandstone palace designed in Indo-Saracenic style.
  • Camel Research Farm: Learn about the ship of the desert at the National Research Centre on Camel.

8. Mount Abu

  • Dilwara Temples: Exquisitely carved Jain temples known for their architectural perfection.
  • Nakki Lake: A popular recreational spot with boating facilities.
  • Guru Shikhar: The highest peak in the Aravalli Range offering spectacular views.
  • Achalgarh Fort: An ancient fort with beautiful temples and scenic views.

9. Chittorgarh

  • Chittorgarh Fort: India's largest fort and a symbol of Rajput valor, pride, and sacrifice.
  • Vijay Stambh (Tower of Victory): A nine-story tower dedicated to Vishnu.
  • Padmini Palace: Associated with the legendary tale of Queen Padmini.
  • Meera Temple: Dedicated to the mystic poet-saint Mirabai who was devoted to Lord Krishna.

Cultural Heritage of Rajasthan

Folk Music and Dance

  • Ghoomar: Traditional women's dance performed in swirling robes.
  • Kalbeliya: The famous snake dance performed by the Kalbeliya tribe.
  • Bhavai: A daring balancing act performed with several pots on the head.
  • Kathputli: Traditional puppet shows narrating folk tales.
  • Manganiyar and Langa: Communities known for their folk music traditions.

Arts and Crafts

Rajasthan is renowned for its rich tradition of handicrafts:

  • Textiles: Bandhani (tie and dye), Leheriya, block printing, Zari embroidery, and Bagru prints.
  • Blue Pottery: Distinctive pottery made from quartz and not clay, originating from Persia.
  • Miniature Paintings: Various schools including Bundi, Kota, Kishangarh, and Mewar.
  • Jewelry: Kundan, Meenakari, and silver jewelry from Jaipur.
  • Carpets and Dhurries: Hand-knotted carpets from Jaipur and dhurries from Bikaner.
  • Leather Work: Especially from Jodhpur and Jaisalmer.
  • Wooden Furniture: Carved and painted furniture from Jodhpur and Shekhawati.

Cuisine

Rajasthani cuisine is influenced by both the war-like lifestyle of its inhabitants and the availability of ingredients in this arid region:

  • Dal Baati Churma: Baked wheat balls served with dal and sweetened crushed wheat.
  • Gatte ki Sabzi: Gram flour dumplings in spicy yogurt gravy.
  • Laal Maas: Fiery red meat curry traditionally made with wild game.
  • Ker Sangri: A dish made from desert berries and beans.
  • Pyaaz Kachori: Deep-fried pastry filled with spicy onion filling.
  • Sweets: Ghevar, Mawa Kachori, Gajak, and Choorma.

Festivals of Rajasthan

  • Pushkar Camel Fair (November): One of the world's largest camel fairs.
  • Desert Festival, Jaisalmer (February): Showcasing folk performances, camel races, and turban-tying competitions.
  • Gangaur (March-April): A festival dedicated to Goddess Gauri, celebrated by women with special significance in Jaipur.
  • Teej (August): Celebrated by women with swings, songs, and prayers for marital bliss.
  • Elephant Festival, Jaipur (March): Featuring decorated elephants, races, and polo matches.
  • Magnetic Fields Festival, Alsisar: A contemporary music festival held in a heritage property.
  • Kite Festival (January): Especially vibrant in Jaipur, with the sky filled with colorful kites.

Best Time to Visit

October to March is the ideal time to visit Rajasthan when the weather is pleasant and comfortable for sightseeing. Summers (April to June) can be extremely hot, with temperatures exceeding 45°C in some areas. The monsoon season (July to September) brings some relief, though rainfall is limited in this largely arid state.

Travel Tips:

  • Carry light cotton clothes for summer, and include a light jacket or shawl for winter evenings which can get quite cool.
  • Stay hydrated, especially when visiting during summer months.
  • Respect local customs and dress modestly when visiting religious sites.
  • Book accommodation in advance if traveling during peak tourist season or festival times.
  • Consider hiring a local guide to better understand the rich history and culture of the monuments.
  • Try the local cuisine, but be cautious with street food if you have a sensitive stomach.
  • Bargain at local markets - it's expected and part of the shopping experience.
  • For the best desert experience in Jaisalmer, consider staying overnight at a desert camp.
