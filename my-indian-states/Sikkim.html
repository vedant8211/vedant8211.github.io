The Rich History of Sikkim

Sikkim's history is a fascinating blend of indigenous cultures, Buddhist traditions, and more recent political developments:

Ancient Period

The earliest inhabitants of Sikkim were the Lepchas (also known as Rongpa, meaning "ravine folk"). The Lepchas practiced shamanism and nature worship. According to their legends, they originated from the Kanchenjunga mountain and Mount Khangchendzonga remains sacred to them.

The Namgyal Dynasty (1642-1975)

Sikkim's monarchy was established in 1642 when Phuntsog Namgyal was consecrated as the first Chogyal (king) of Sikkim by three Tibetan Lamas at Yuksom. The Namgyal dynasty ruled Sikkim for over 330 years. During this period, Sikkim faced invasions from Nepal, Tibet, and Bhutan at various times.

British Influence

In the mid-19th century, Sikkim allied with British India against Nepal. The Anglo-Sikkimese Treaty of 1861 made Sikkim a British protectorate, giving the British control over Sikkim's external affairs and defense. The British used Sikkim as a gateway for trade with Tibet and helped establish the tea industry in the region.

Independence and Merger with India

After India's independence in 1947, Sikkim maintained its protectorate status under a treaty signed in 1950. However, growing political movements within Sikkim called for greater democratic rights and closer ties with India. In 1975, after a referendum in which 97.5% of voters supported abolishing the monarchy, Sikkim officially became the 22nd state of India on May 16, 1975.

Modern Sikkim

Since joining India, Sikkim has transformed significantly. It has become a pioneer in organic farming, with the state declared fully organic in 2016. Sikkim has also made remarkable progress in education, healthcare, and sustainable development, often ranking high in various human development indices within India.

Top Places to Visit in Sikkim

1. Gangtok - The Capital City

A picturesque hill station and Sikkim's capital:

  • Rumtek Monastery: One of the most important and largest monasteries in Sikkim, representing the seat of the Karmapa Lama in India.
  • Namgyal Institute of Tibetology: A research center focused on Buddhist philosophy and religion with a museum housing rare artifacts.
  • Enchey Monastery: A 200-year-old monastery with beautiful paintings and carvings.
  • MG Marg: A lively pedestrian-only boulevard with shops, cafes, and cultural spaces.
  • Ganesh Tok and Hanuman Tok: Viewpoints offering panoramic vistas of Kanchenjunga and surrounding mountains.
  • Banjhakri Falls: A beautiful waterfall set amidst landscaped gardens with ethnic sculptures.

2. Nathula Pass

A historic mountain pass on the Indo-China border:

  • Trade Route: Part of the ancient Silk Road connecting India and Tibet.
  • High Altitude: Located at 14,140 feet above sea level with spectacular views.
  • Historical Significance: Site of the 1962 Indo-China conflict, now a peaceful border post.
  • Tsomgo Lake (Changu Lake): A sacred glacial lake on the way to Nathula.
  • Baba Mandir: A shrine dedicated to a soldier, Harbhajan Singh, believed to protect the area.

3. Pelling

  • Pemayangtse Monastery: A premier monastery of the Nyingmapa order, known for its intricate woodcarvings.
  • Rabdentse Ruins: The remains of the second capital of the former Kingdom of Sikkim.
  • Kanchenjunga Falls: A magnificent waterfall with stunning views.
  • Khecheopalri Lake: A sacred lake believed to fulfill wishes, known as the "Wishing Lake."
  • Singshore Bridge: Asia's second-highest suspension bridge.

4. Yuksom

  • Historical Significance: The first capital of Sikkim where the first Chogyal was crowned.
  • Dubdi Monastery: Sikkim's oldest monastery established in 1701.
  • Norbugang Chorten: The coronation site of the first King with the coronation throne.
  • Trekking Base: Starting point for the popular Dzongri-Goecha La trek.
  • Kathok Lake: A serene, sacred lake surrounded by forest.

5. Lachung and Lachen

  • Lachung: A picturesque village known for its unique local self-governing body called the Dzumsa.
  • Yumthang Valley: Known as the "Valley of Flowers," with hot springs and vast meadows.
  • Zero Point (Yumesamdong): The last civilian outpost near the Chinese border with snow-covered landscapes.
  • Lachen: Gateway to North Sikkim and the sacred Gurudongmar Lake.
  • Gurudongmar Lake: One of the world's highest lakes at 17,800 feet, considered sacred by Buddhists and Sikhs.

6. Ravangla

  • Buddha Park: Features a 130-foot statue of Buddha.
  • Ralang Monastery: Home to rare thangkas and religious artifacts.
  • Maenam Wildlife Sanctuary: Rich biodiversity with panoramic views from Maenam Hill.
  • Temi Tea Garden: Sikkim's only tea estate producing world-renowned organic tea.

7. Zuluk

A remote village on the historic Silk Route with spectacular landscapes and the famous zigzag roads with 32 hairpin bends.

Cultural Heritage of Sikkim

Traditional Festivals

  • Losar: Tibetan New Year celebrated with prayers, dance, and feasting.
  • Saga Dawa: Celebrates Buddha's birth, enlightenment, and parinirvana.
  • Lhabab Duechen: Commemorates Buddha's descent from heaven.
  • Pang Lhabsol: Unique to Sikkim, worshipping Mount Kanchenjunga as the guardian deity.
  • Dashain and Tihar: Major Nepali Hindu festivals celebrated widely in Sikkim.

Music and Dance

  • Cham Dance: Masked religious dance performed by monks in monasteries.
  • Maruni: Folk dance performed during Dashain and Tihar.
  • Tamang Selo: Traditional dance of the Tamang community.
  • Singhi Chham: The snow lion dance symbolizing Tibet's independence.

Traditional Crafts

  • Thangka Painting: Religious scroll paintings depicting Buddhist deities.
  • Wood Carving: Intricate carvings seen in monasteries and traditional houses.
  • Carpet Weaving: Hand-woven carpets with distinctive patterns.
  • Bamboo Crafts: Everyday items and decorative pieces made from bamboo.

Cuisine

Sikkimese cuisine is influenced by Tibetan, Nepali, and indigenous traditions:

  • Momos: Steamed or fried dumplings filled with meat or vegetables.
  • Thukpa: Noodle soup with vegetables and meat.
  • Gundruk: Fermented leafy green vegetable, a popular side dish.
  • Phagshapa: Pork cooked with radishes and dried chilies.
  • Sel Roti: Sweet rice bread, typically prepared during festivals.
  • Chhang: A mildly alcoholic brew made from fermented millet.

Natural Wonders

Flora and Fauna

Sikkim is a biodiversity hotspot with over 4,500 species of flowering plants, 550 bird species, and numerous rare and endangered animals:

  • Khangchendzonga National Park: A UNESCO World Heritage Site home to snow leopards, Himalayan tahrs, and red pandas.
  • Orchids: Sikkim hosts approximately 600 species of orchids, making it an orchid paradise.
  • Rhododendrons: The state flower, with 36 species found in the region.
  • Medicinal Plants: Over 424 species of plants with medicinal properties.

Adventure Activities

  • Trekking: Popular routes include Goecha La, Dzongri, Green Lake, and the Singalila Ridge.
  • River Rafting: Thrilling experiences on the Teesta and Rangit rivers.
  • Mountain Biking: Challenging routes through mountain terrain.
  • Paragliding: Available in places like Gangtok and Pelling.
  • Yak Safari: A unique way to explore the high-altitude regions.

Best Time to Visit

March to May (spring) and October to mid-December (autumn) are the best times to visit Sikkim. Spring brings beautiful rhododendron blooms, while autumn offers clear skies and spectacular mountain views. Summer (June to September) brings monsoon rains with lush greenery but also landslides. Winter (December to February) is cold with snowfall in higher regions, offering its own unique charm for snow enthusiasts.

Travel Tips:

  • Foreign nationals need a Protected Area Permit (PAP) to visit certain areas of Sikkim.
  • Indian tourists need an Inner Line Permit for North Sikkim.
  • Carry warm clothes even in summer as temperatures can drop unexpectedly.
  • Altitude sickness can be an issue, so acclimatize properly and stay hydrated.
  • Respect local customs, especially in monasteries and sacred places.
  • Network connectivity can be limited in remote areas, so plan accordingly.
  • Plastic bags are banned in Sikkim as part of its environmental conservation efforts.
  • Public transport is limited, so consider hiring a local taxi for convenience.
